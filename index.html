Opening · Block 1

A paper that argued planning is useless

In 2016, in a class called Strategy, we read a theoretical paper arguing that humans can't possibly account for all the variables in any given scenario — so what's the point in planning?


It felt counterintuitive. It also felt completely right.


After working in tech since graduating — in companies across Brazil, the UK, Panama, Mexico, and the US — that paper still holds up. Not because planning is worthless, but because the gap between how planning is taught and how it actually happens in the market is enormous.

The takeaway

No company uses one framework consistently. Most use fragments of several, often inconsistently. The skill isn't knowing the framework — it's knowing when to use which lens.

The real argument

The paper wasn't saying don't plan. It was saying: stop mistaking the plan for the territory. The process of planning forces conversations that would never happen otherwise. That's the value — not the document.

Why tech experience matters here

Most planning theory was written by academics who study companies. Tech companies write the theory by shipping products. The feedback loop is faster, the failure is more visible, and the frameworks are stress-tested in real time.

Context · Block 2

These frameworks were built
for a different world

Tech companies

Where these tools were born

  • → Clear, identifiable users
  • → Fast feedback loops (days, not years)
  • → Abundant capital and iteration budget
  • → Metrics that are easy to define and track
  • → Teams with clear ownership
Assentamento Florescer

Where you'll apply them today

  • → Diffuse stakeholders: families, INCRA, local gov, extensionists
  • → Slow feedback (policies play out over years)
  • → Severe resource and institutional constraints
  • → Outcomes hard to quantify (dignity, permanence, cohesion)
  • → Overlapping crises — no single lever
That's not a reason to reject these tools. It's a reason to use them critically.

Tool 01 · Double Diamond · 15 minutes

Double Diamond — ideate before you commit

Stay in the problem space a little longer than feels comfortable. Generate more ideas than you think you need — before you start cutting.

Step 1 · Diverge (8 min)

Post-its, one idea each. Generate problems (what's broken?) and interventions (what could be done?). Quantity over quality. Aim for 8–12 per person. No evaluation yet.

Step 2 · Converge (7 min)

Group similar post-its on the big paper. Label clusters. Identify 5–8 distinct intervention areas. Output goes into the next tool. You're not choosing yet — you're organizing.

Starter prompts for the settlement
  • ? What keeps families from staying on the land?
  • ? What would change if water access was solved?
  • ? Who needs to change their behavior for this to work?
  • ? What could INCRA do that it currently doesn't?
  • ? What's the one fix that unlocks everything else?
Your output

A cluster map on big paper with 5–8 labeled idea groups, ready to plot on the Impact vs. Feasibility matrix.

Facilitator note

If stuck: "What's the one thing that, if fixed, makes everything else easier?" That usually unlocks it.

Tool 02 · Impact vs. Feasibility · 15 minutes

Impact vs. Feasibility — map your bets

Take the ideas from your Double Diamond and plot them on a 2×2 grid. Axes: Impact on families (Y) and Feasibility given real constraints (X).

"Feasibility" means: institutional capacity, INCRA policy, water access, land conditions, community readiness — not just money.

Step 1 · Plot (8 min)

Draw the 2×2 on your big paper. Place each cluster from the Double Diamond as a sticky note. Discuss as you place — disagreements are the point.

Step 2 · Dot vote (7 min)

Each person gets 3 dots. Rules: you cannot place all 3 on the same idea. The idea with most dots is your bet. Take it to the Impact Map.

Honest caveat

"Impact" and "feasibility" are subjective. Use this to start a conversation — not to end one. Disagreement is your most important data point.

Tool 03 · Impact Map · 20 minutes

Impact Map — who does what?

You have your idea. Now: who needs to act for it to work, and what do you need to deliver to make that happen?

By Gojko Adzic (2012). Every deliverable must trace back to a behavior change in a real actor. If you can't draw that line, the deliverable doesn't belong on the map.

Why?
The goal. Measurable. Time-bound. What does success look like in 3 years?
Who?
Families
INCRA
Extensionists
Local gov
How?
Behavior changes needed from each actor to reach the goal
What?
Deliverables that enable those behavior changes
How to run it (20 min)
  • 1 Write your goal (Why) — specific and time-bound
  • 2 List all actors (Who) who can help or hinder
  • 3 For each actor, write the behavior change needed (How)
  • 4 For each behavior change, write what to build or provide (What)
  • 5 Challenge every "What": if removed, does the behavior change still happen?
Source

Adzic, Impact Mapping (2012) · impactmapping.org

Watch for

Teams default to listing deliverables (What) before asking How. The behavior change column is where most planning actually fails.
